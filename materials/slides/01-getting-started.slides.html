Scientific Notation

When studying chemistry, you will frequently encounter numbers that are either extremely large or incredibly small. For instance, the number of atoms in a small sample of matter (Avogadro’s number) is 602,200,000,000,000,000,000,000602,200,000,000,000,000,000,000602,200,000,000,000,000,000,000. Conversely, the mass of a single electron is approximately 0.00000000000000000000000000000091090.00000000000000000000000000000091090.0000000000000000000000000000009109 kilograms. Writing out these numbers in their standard form is not only tedious but also increases the chances of making errors in calculations or transcription.

Scientific notation is a standardized way to express these very large or very small numbers concisely. It simplifies calculations, makes numbers easier to read, and helps avoid errors by reducing the number of zeros you need to write.

The Format

A number written in scientific notation has two parts:

N×10xN \times 10^xN×10x

Where:

  • NNN is the coefficient (or mantissa). This number must be greater than or equal to 1 and less than 10 (1≤N<101 \le N < 101≤N<10). It contains all the significant figures of the original number.
  • 10x10^x10x is the base 10 raised to an exponent (xxx).
    • The exponent xxx is an integer (a whole number, which can be positive or negative).
    • A positive exponent (x>0x > 0x>0) indicates a large number (the decimal point was moved to the left).
    • A negative exponent (x<0x < 0x<0) indicates a small number (the decimal point was moved to the right).

Examples:

  • Avogadro’s number: 6.022×10236.022 \times 10^{23}6.022×1023 (Here, N=6.022N = 6.022N=6.022, and x=23x = 23x=23)
  • Mass of an electron: 9.109×10−319.109 \times 10^{-31}9.109×10−31 kg (Here, N=9.109N = 9.109N=9.109, and x=−31x = -31x=−31)

Converting to Scientific Notation

To convert a number from its standard form to scientific notation:

  1. Locate the decimal point. If no decimal is explicitly written (e.g., in a whole number like 500), assume it’s at the end of the number (500.500.500.).
  2. Move the decimal point until there is only one non-zero digit to its left. This new position defines the coefficient NNN.
  3. Count the number of places you moved the decimal point. This count will be the absolute value of your exponent xxx.
  4. Determine the sign of the exponent:
    • If you moved the decimal point to the left (because the original number was large), the exponent is positive.
    • If you moved the decimal point to the right (because the original number was small), the exponent is negative.

Example 1: Converting a large number Convert 123,000123,000123,000 to scientific notation.

  1. Original number: 123,000.123,000.123,000. (decimal is implied at the end)
  2. Move decimal to get one non-zero digit to its left: 1.230001.230001.23000
  3. Number of places moved: 5 places to the left.
  4. Exponent is positive. Therefore, 123,000=1.23×105123,000 = 1.23 \times 10^5123,000=1.23×105

Example 2: Converting a small number Convert 0.0000450.0000450.000045 to scientific notation.

  1. Original number: 0.0000450.0000450.000045
  2. Move decimal to get one non-zero digit to its left: 4.54.54.5
  3. Number of places moved: 5 places to the right.
  4. Exponent is negative. Therefore, 0.000045=4.5×10−50.000045 = 4.5 \times 10^{-5}0.000045=4.5×10−5

Converting from Scientific Notation to Standard Form

To convert a number from scientific notation back to its standard form:

  1. Examine the exponent (xxx).
  2. Move the decimal point in the coefficient (NNN) by the number of places indicated by the exponent.
    • If the exponent is positive, move the decimal point to the right. Add zeros as placeholders if you run out of digits.
    • If the exponent is negative, move the decimal point to the left. Add zeros as placeholders if you run out of digits.

Example 1: Positive exponent Convert 3.14×1033.14 \times 10^33.14×103 to standard form.

  1. The exponent is 333 (positive).
  2. Move the decimal point 3 places to the right: 3.14→31.4→314.→3140.3.14 \rightarrow 31.4 \rightarrow 314. \rightarrow 3140.3.14→31.4→314.→3140. Therefore, 3.14×103=31403.14 \times 10^3 = 31403.14×103=3140

Example 2: Negative exponent Convert 7.8×10−47.8 \times 10^{-4}7.8×10−4 to standard form.

  1. The exponent is −4-4−4 (negative).
  2. Move the decimal point 4 places to the left: 7.8→0.78→0.078→0.0078→0.000787.8 \rightarrow 0.78 \rightarrow 0.078 \rightarrow 0.0078 \rightarrow 0.000787.8→0.78→0.078→0.0078→0.00078 Therefore, 7.8×10−4=0.000787.8 \times 10^{-4} = 0.000787.8×10−4=0.00078

Le Chatelier’s Principle

Le Chatelier’s Principle is a powerful tool that helps us predict how a system at chemical equilibrium will respond to a disturbance, or “stress.” It states that if a change of condition (stress) is applied to a system in equilibrium, the system will shift in a direction that relieves the stress and re-establishes a new equilibrium. Think of it as the system trying to “fight back” against the change to restore balance.

Let’s look at common types of stress and how they affect equilibrium:

1. Change in Concentration If you add more of a reactant or product, the system will shift to consume the added substance. If you remove a reactant or product, the system will shift to produce more of that substance.

  • Consider the Haber-Bosch process for ammonia synthesis: NX2(g)+3 HX2(g)⇌2 NHX3(g)\ce{N2(g) + 3H2(g) <=> 2NH3(g)}NX2​(g)+3HX2​(g)​2NHX3​(g)
    • If you add more NX2\ce{N2}NX2​ (a reactant), the equilibrium will shift to the right (towards products) to consume the extra NX2\ce{N2}NX2​, producing more NHX3\ce{NH3}NHX3​.
    • If you remove NHX3\ce{NH3}NHX3​ (a product, e.g., by liquefying it), the equilibrium will shift to the right to replace the lost NHX3\ce{NH3}NHX3​, producing more NHX3\ce{NH3}NHX3​.
    • If you add more NHX3\ce{NH3}NHX3​, the equilibrium will shift to the left (towards reactants) to consume the extra NHX3\ce{NH3}NHX3​.

2. Change in Pressure (for gaseous systems) Changes in pressure primarily affect reactions involving gases, especially when there’s a different number of gas moles on each side of the equation.

  • Increasing pressure (e.g., by decreasing the volume of the container) will cause the equilibrium to shift towards the side with fewer moles of gas to reduce the overall pressure.
  • Decreasing pressure (e.g., by increasing the volume) will cause the equilibrium to shift towards the side with more moles of gas to increase the overall pressure.
  • Using the Haber-Bosch process again: NX2(g)+3 HX2(g)⇌2 NHX3(g)\ce{N2(g) + 3H2(g) <=> 2NH3(g)}NX2​(g)+3HX2​(g)​2NHX3​(g)
    • Reactant side: 111 mole NX2\ce{N2}NX2​ + 333 moles HX2\ce{H2}HX2​ = 444 moles of gas.
    • Product side: 222 moles NHX3\ce{NH3}NHX3​ = 222 moles of gas.
    • If you increase the pressure, the equilibrium will shift to the right (towards 222 moles of NHX3\ce{NH3}NHX3​) because there are fewer moles of gas on the product side, which helps relieve the pressure stress.
    • If you decrease the pressure, the equilibrium will shift to the left (towards 444 moles of reactants) to produce more gas moles and increase the pressure.
    • Note: Adding an inert gas (one not involved in the reaction, like argon) to a constant-volume system does not change the partial pressures of the reacting gases, and thus does not shift the equilibrium.

3. Change in Temperature Temperature changes are unique because they affect the value of the equilibrium constant (KKK) itself, not just the position of equilibrium. To understand the shift, treat heat as either a reactant or a product.

  • Endothermic Reactions (absorb heat): Reactants+Heat⇌Products\ce{\text{Reactants} + \text{Heat} <=> \text{Products}}Reactants+Heat​Products
    • If you increase the temperature (add heat), the equilibrium shifts to the right (towards products) to consume the added heat.
    • If you decrease the temperature (remove heat), the equilibrium shifts to the left (towards reactants) to produce more heat.
  • Exothermic Reactions (release heat): Reactants⇌Products+Heat\ce{\text{Reactants} <=> \text{Products} + \text{Heat}}Reactants​Products+Heat
    • If you increase the temperature (add heat), the equilibrium shifts to the left (towards reactants) to consume the added heat.
    • If you decrease the temperature (remove heat), the equilibrium shifts to the right (towards products) to produce more heat.

What Doesn’t Shift Equilibrium?

  • Catalysts speed up both the forward and reverse reactions equally. They help the system reach equilibrium faster, but they do not change the position of the equilibrium or the values of equilibrium constants. The system will reach the same equilibrium composition, just more quickly.
